Broader Context

Jewish Women in American Sport  ·  The "New Jew" and Muscular Judaism

Part I  ·  Borish (1999)

Jewish Women in American Sport: The Institutional Story

Charlotte Epstein and Andrea Gyarmati are remarkable figures — but they do not exist in a vacuum. To understand what their athletic careers meant, we need to understand the broader landscape of Jewish women's participation in American sport, and the institutions that simultaneously enabled and constrained that participation. Historian Linda Borish provides this landscape in her 1999 article "Athletic Activities of Various Kinds," which traces Jewish American women's sport participation from the 1880s through the 1940s.

Borish's central argument is that Jewish women's access to sport during this period was shaped by a network of Jewish communal institutions: settlement houses, Young Women's Hebrew Associations (YWHAs), Young Men's Hebrew Associations (YMHAs), and organizations like the Chicago Hebrew Institute. These institutions played an ambiguous role. On one hand, they opened up physical spaces — pools, gymnasiums, courts — where Jewish women could develop athletic skills they would have been denied in mainstream American institutions that were often hostile to or simply uninterested in accommodating Jewish women. On the other hand, these same institutions were not free from the gender ideologies of their era. They reflected and reinforced certain assumptions about what kinds of athletic activity were appropriate for women, and under what conditions women's physical culture should be pursued.

Women competing in a separate ladies' swimming tank, ca. early twentieth century. From Borish, "Athletic Activities of Various Kinds" (1999).

This image captures what Borish's scholarship describes: Jewish women had access to swimming, but access on terms set by others — separate tanks, separate rules, separate audiences. The physical space itself encoded the limits of their inclusion.

"The story of Jewish women in American sport remains unexplored in women's history, sport history, and American Jewish history. The ways Jewish women participated in sport and physical recreation in Jewish settlement houses and Jewish Ys within the immigrant and larger American culture indicate gender, class, ethnic, and religious considerations, little studied by women's sport and health history scholars."
— Linda J. Borish, "Athletic Activities of Various Kinds" (1999)

Borish's scholarship reveals that the barriers Jewish women faced in sport were not primarily a matter of individual prejudice — though prejudice certainly existed — but were structural. They were embedded in the rules of governing bodies like the AAU, in the physical design of facilities that prioritized male athletic spaces, and in the cultural assumptions that made women's serious athletic aspiration seem anomalous or even inappropriate.

Institutions

The YWHA: Access and Its Limits

The Young Women's Hebrew Association and its sibling organization the YMHA deserve particular attention, because they illustrate the complexity of what it meant for Jewish women to have "access" to sport in this era. Borish documents that these organizations did provide meaningful athletic opportunities. Swimming classes, gymnastic programs, and physical culture activities were offered to Jewish women in YWHA settings when those same women might have had no access to comparable facilities or instruction elsewhere.

But access was conditioned. The physical culture promoted by these institutions was often framed in terms of health, refinement, and appropriate femininity — not in terms of competitive excellence or athletic achievement for its own sake. Women were encouraged to be active, but within limits that reinforced rather than challenged prevailing gender norms. A Jewish woman who wanted to compete seriously, to measure herself against others, to pursue victory as a goal in itself, was pushing against the expectations not just of the broader American athletic world but sometimes of her own community's institutions.

The Palais du Champ de Mars, featuring separate swimming tanks for men and women. From Borish, "Athletic Activities of Various Kinds" (1999).

The architecture of separation was not incidental. Designing separate tanks into the building itself made gendered exclusion structural rather than merely procedural — something that had to be physically dismantled, not simply argued against.

Why This Context Matters for Epstein

Borish's 1999 scholarship provides the essential background for understanding why Charlotte Epstein's founding of the Women's Swimming Association in 1917 was such a significant act. Epstein did not create an institution that fit within the existing framework of Jewish women's physical culture — she created one that explicitly challenged and exceeded it. The WSA was not about health and refinement; it was about competitive excellence, Olympic ambition, and institutional change. In the context Borish describes, this represented a genuinely radical departure from the norms that governed Jewish women's sport in her era.

Borish's work also helps explain why the barriers Epstein faced were so stubborn. They were not simply the product of individual male chauvinism at the AAU, though that chauvinism was real. They were supported by a broader cultural framework — spanning Jewish communal institutions, mainstream athletic governance, and American gender ideology — that made women's serious athletic competition seem problematic from multiple directions at once. Epstein was fighting not just the AAU but a cultural consensus.

Part II  ·  Idels (2022)

The "New Jew" and the Problem of Athletic Identity

The story of Jewish women in sport takes on a different and particularly revealing dimension when we introduce the concept of "Muscular Judaism" and the figure of the "New Jew" — a Zionist ideological project that promoted Jewish physical strength as a form of cultural and political assertion. Ofer Idels examines this project in his 2022 article "How to Lose Gracefully in an Internationally Selfish World," which focuses on gender, the "New Jew" ideal, and the way athletic performance was understood (and misrepresented) in interwar Palestine.

The concept of the Muskeljude — the "Muscle Jew" — originated with the Zionist theorist Max Nordau, who argued at the turn of the twentieth century that the Jewish people needed to reclaim their physical vitality. In Nordau's view, centuries of life in the diaspora, confined to ghettos and denied access to outdoor physical activity, had produced a "ghetto Jew" who was physically weak, intellectually overstrained, and psychologically damaged. The Zionist "New Jew" was to be different: physically vigorous, connected to the land, and capable of defending himself and building a new nation.

Hebrew female athletes from Maccabi Eretz Israel carrying the flag at the Women's World Games, London, 1934. From Idels, "How to Lose Gracefully" (2022).

This image is central to Idels's argument. The camera focuses not on the athletes' faces or bodies but on the flag they carry — the national symbol that, in Zionist eyes, was the real point of their presence. The women are there, but they are rendered invisible. What the photograph captures is not athletic identity but national symbolism, and in that substitution lies the paradox Idels identifies: these women were celebrated as Hebrew bodies bearing a Hebrew flag, not as athletes who had trained, competed, and earned their place on that field.

"unlike the athletes of many other societies at the time, the Hebrew athlete was not embraced as a national symbol"
— Ofer Idels, "How to Lose Gracefully" (2022)

The Central Paradox

Strength Without Celebration: The Muscular Judaism Paradox

Here is where Idels's scholarship becomes particularly illuminating: he identifies a central paradox in the Muscular Judaism movement. While the "New Jew" ideology promoted physical strength and athletic excellence as Jewish ideals, it simultaneously resisted celebrating individual athletes — especially female ones — in ways that would have made those ideals concrete and personal.

Idels documents that in interwar Palestine, Jewish athletes who performed well in international competition were often not celebrated as individuals in the way that comparable athletes in other national contexts were. Their achievements were absorbed into a nationalist narrative that erased individual identity in favor of collective symbolism. And this erasure was particularly pronounced for women: female athletes who won in international competition might be acknowledged, but their achievements were frequently framed in terms that diminished their individual athletic agency and subordinated their identities as women to their identities as symbols of the national project.

"In the eyes of the Zionist revolution, the regeneration of the Jewish body was something that occurred in the tilling field and on the battlefield, but not on the sports field. Compared to the purposeful productivity of the halutz, the actions of the athlete appeared frivolous and empty. As a result, sporting heroes (whether male or female) and athletic achievements are absent from the Zionist collective memory of this time."
— Ofer Idels, "How to Lose Gracefully" (2022)

This is a profound tension. The ideological framework that most explicitly celebrated Jewish athletic achievement was also the framework most unable to celebrate Jewish women's athletic achievement on its own terms. Jewish women who competed in the international arena were simultaneously useful symbols for the nationalist project and inconvenient complications for a vision of Jewish physical renewal that had been built, almost entirely, around the figure of the Jewish man.

Synthesis

What the "New Jew" Framework Reveals About Our Subjects

Neither Epstein nor Gyarmati fit neatly into the "New Jew" framework — and that misfitting is analytically significant. Epstein was working in the American context, not the Zionist one, and her advocacy was feminist rather than nationalist in its orientation. She was not arguing that Jewish women should be athletes because it would strengthen the Jewish people. She was arguing that women — including Jewish women — had the right to compete, to be recognized, and to be excellent on their own terms. This is a meaningfully different claim than the "New Jew" ideology advanced.

Gyarmati, competing as a Hungarian Jewish woman in international swimming in the 1960s and 1970s, also occupied a position outside the "New Jew" framework. Her Jewishness was not primarily a Zionist identity; she was European, post-Holocaust, and navigating Jewish identity in a communist Hungarian context that had its own complex relationship to Jewish difference. The "New Jew" ideal, built for Palestine, offered her no framework at all for understanding her own athletic identity.

The key insight: The "New Jew" paradox — promoting strength while failing to celebrate individual athletes, especially women — was not a Palestinian or Zionist problem alone. It reflected a broader pattern in Jewish cultural life: the tendency to value Jewish achievement abstractly while struggling to honor the specific, embodied, gendered people who actually produced it.

Both Epstein and Gyarmati, in different ways and different contexts, were producing exactly the kind of Jewish athletic achievement that the "New Jew" ideal claimed to celebrate. Neither received from their respective cultural contexts the full recognition their achievements warranted. The reasons for that gap — institutional, gendered, ideological — are what this project has been investigating from the beginning.
