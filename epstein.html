Profile

Charlotte Epstein

1884–1938  ·  The Mother of Women's Swimming in America

Biographical Overview

Who Was Charlotte Epstein?

At a Glance

Born 1884, New York City
Died 1938
Background Jewish American
Profession Court stenographer, activist, sports organizer
Founded Women's Swimming Association of New York (WSA), 1917
Olympics Manager, US women's swim team, 1920, 1924, 1928

Charlotte Epstein was not a swimmer. She was something more consequential, a builder. Working as a court stenographer in New York City in the early twentieth century, Epstein recognized that women's access to competitive swimming was being systematically blocked by male-dominated athletic institutions, and she decided to do something about it. In 1917, she founded the Women's Swimming Association of New York (WSA), an organization that would become the training ground for some of the most celebrated American Olympic swimmers in the 1920s.

The Jewish Women's Archive describes Epstein as the "Mother of Women's Swimming in America", a title that captures both her foundational role in developing the sport and the maternal, protective advocacy she provided for the women under her care. She did not simply create space for female athletes. She fought, negotiated, and organized to ensure that space was fair, dignified, and permanent.

The 1924 U.S. Women's Olympic Swimming Team, which included athletes trained under Charlotte Epstein and the Women's Swimming Association of New York.

Epstein served as manager of the U.S. women's swim team across three consecutive Olympic Games — 1920, 1924, and 1928 — making her the first American woman to hold that role. The women in this photograph are the product of the institution she built.

Institutional Resistance

Fighting the AAU

The Amateur Athletic Union (AAU) governed competitive athletics in the United States, and its approach to women's sport in the early twentieth century was very restrictive. Female swimmers were subject to rules that had little to do with athleticism and everything to do with policing women's bodies and appearances. The AAU required women to compete in stockings and bloomers, which were designed to preserve modesty, and simultaneously made competitive swimming nearly impossible.

Epstein challenged these restrictions directly. As historian Linda Borish documents in her 2004 study of Epstein's legacy, Epstein fought for women's right to wear practical racing suits, to compete in legitimate venues, and to be recognized as serious athletes rather than curiosities. Borish writes that Epstein was instrumental in "the physical emancipation of women in aquatic sports," and that her advocacy connected the practical demands of athletic competition to the broader feminist project of claiming women's physical autonomy in public life.

"Charlotte Epstein serves as a symbol of the critical efforts of a Jewish sportswoman to improve the competitive opportunities and quest for physical emancipation of American women using their bodies in aquatic sports."
— Linda J. Borish, "The Cradle of American Champions" (2004)

What makes Epstein's fight particularly significant is that she was not appealing for a modest reform within an otherwise fair system. She was exposing and dismantling a structure that had been designed to keep women out. The AAU's rules were not neutral; they were gendered instruments of exclusion dressed up as standards.

Women's Rights

Swimming as Suffrage

One of the most striking aspects of Epstein's work is how deliberately she linked athletic advocacy to the broader women's suffrage movement. Borish's research notes that WSA swimmers sometimes wore "Votes for Women" sashes at competitive meets. This practice made the political subtext of women's athletic participation explicit. The pool was not just a place to train; it was a place to make an argument.

"The physical freedom of women's bodies in swimming went together with the political freedom of seeking the vote for women in the 1910s, articulated by Epstein and reform-minded National Women's Life-Saving League team-mates."
— Linda J. Borish, "The Cradle of American Champions" (2004)
The swim contest "concluded with a suffrage rescue race, in which all the starters wore 'votes for women' sashes."
— New York Tribune, July 1915, quoted in Borish (2004)

This connection was not incidental. The suffrage movement was deeply invested in challenging the ideology of female physical weakness. This stems from the Victorian notion that women were naturally fragile and unsuited to the rigors of public life, politics, or strenuous physical activity. Female athletes, in this context, were walking counterarguments. Every race a woman swam, every record she set, was a public refutation of the claim that women's bodies limited their capacities.

For Epstein, this meant that developing a world-class women's swim team was itself a form of political activism. When her WSA swimmers stood on Olympic podiums in 1920, 1924, and 1928, they were not just winning medals. They were accumulating evidence.

Identity

Jewish Identity and the Body

Epstein's Jewish identity adds another layer of significance to her work. As Borish notes, one of the dominant stereotypes that Jewish women faced in this era was the notion of physical weakness — the assumption that Jewish women were not suited to athletic competition, that their bodies were somehow less capable or less robust than those of other women. This stereotype was bound up with both antisemitism and with specifically gendered ideas about Jewish femininity.

Epstein's work challenged this assumption not through argument but through performance. Her WSA produced Olympic champions. The women she trained and managed won medals at three consecutive Olympic Games. In doing so, they did not simply win races; they dismantled a cultural narrative about what Jewish women's bodies could do.

The Intersection of Gender and Jewish Identity

Borish's scholarship frames Epstein's significance in terms of what she calls the "intertwining" of Jewish identity with athletic advocacy. For Epstein, fighting for women's access to competitive swimming was not separate from being Jewish — it was an expression of a community's claim to full participation in American public life. Jewish women who won Olympic medals were not just athletes; they were arguments about who belonged in America, who deserved recognition, and whose bodies counted.

This is the analytical key to understanding Epstein: her fight was simultaneously a fight about gender (women deserved to compete), about Jewish identity (Jewish women deserved to be seen as capable), and about American civic belonging (Jewish women were Americans whose achievements merited institutional recognition and celebration).

"Charlotte Epstein through her determined effort and leadership helped to shatter a stereotype about Jewish women's lack of physical capability and participation in sport."
— Linda J. Borish, "The Cradle of American Champions" (2004)

Olympic Legacy

Three Olympics, A Lasting Impact

Epstein managed the United States women's swimming team at the 1920 Antwerp Olympics, the 1924 Paris Olympics, and the 1928 Amsterdam Olympics — making her the first American woman to serve in this role across three consecutive Games. Her athletes dominated. The WSA produced swimmers who won multiple gold medals, setting records and establishing American women as among the finest competitive swimmers in the world.

What is remarkable about this legacy, given what Epstein had to fight through to build it, is how quickly the institutional obstacles that had once seemed immovable gave way once women actually competed. Once women demonstrated on the Olympic stage what they could do, the argument for their exclusion became much harder to sustain. Epstein understood this strategic logic intuitively: the best argument for women's inclusion was women's excellence.

Charlotte Epstein died in 1938 — a year before the war that would define the twentieth century's relationship with Jewish life in ways she could not have fully anticipated. But the institutions she built, the athletes she trained, and the arguments she made through their achievements outlasted her. The women who competed in those early Olympic pools were doing something that would echo forward in time: they were claiming space.
