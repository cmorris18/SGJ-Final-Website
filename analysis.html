Thematic Analysis

Why This Matters

Swimming, Jewish Identity, and the Politics of the Body

The Central Question

Why Swimming? The Pool as Political Space

Of all the sports a project about gender, Jewish identity, and resistance could examine, why swimming? The answer is not incidental. Swimming is uniquely suited to illuminate the questions this project has been pursuing, because the pool is a uniquely contested space in the history of gender and the body.

First, competitive swimming requires a degree of bodily exposure that most other sports do not. Swimmers compete in minimal clothing, in public, observed by judges, coaches, and spectators. This means that debates about what women swimmers could wear — the swimsuit controversies Epstein fought the AAU over in the 1910s and 1920s — were never really just debates about clothing. They were debates about whether women's bodies could be displayed, how, and under whose authority. The swimsuit was a proxy for the woman. To control what she wore in the water was to control how she was seen, and by extension, what she was permitted to be.

Second, water has historically been a gendered space. Public pools, in the early twentieth century, were often sex-segregated or male-dominated. The very act of a woman entering the pool — of claiming water as a space in which she could compete, train, and excel — was a territorial claim. Epstein understood this. Her WSA was not simply a swim club; it was an assertion that women had a right to inhabit athletic space, to take up lane room, to be timed and ranked and celebrated.

"Charlotte Epstein illuminates the links between athletic and political activities in expanding the swimming opportunities for women."
— Linda J. Borish, "The Cradle of American Champions" (2004)

Comparative Analysis

Epstein and Gyarmati: What They Share

Charlotte Epstein

  • Jewish woman navigating male-dominated institutions
  • Fighting for women's access to competitive sport
  • American context: AAU, Olympic governance
  • Early twentieth century (1910s–1930s)
  • Identity: feminist, Jewish, advocate
  • Agency: fighting into the institution

Andrea Gyarmati

  • Jewish woman navigating family legacy and national sport culture
  • Competing under the pressure of inherited excellence
  • Hungarian/European context: Olympic sport, Holocaust aftermath
  • Mid-to-late twentieth century (1960s–1970s)
  • Identity: athlete, daughter, Jewish, Hungarian
  • Agency: choosing to walk out of the institution

Despite their very different contexts — different eras, different countries, different relationships to competitive swimming — Epstein and Gyarmati share several analytically significant features. Both were Jewish women whose relationship to sport was shaped by forces that had little to do with their individual talent and everything to do with structures of gender, identity, and institutional power. Both made choices about their athletic participation that were simultaneously personal and political. And both, in different ways, exercised a form of agency that challenged the terms on which they were expected to operate.

They also share something subtler: neither was simply a victim of the systems they navigated. Epstein actively organized, strategized, and won concessions from a system designed to exclude her athletes. Gyarmati, too, was not passive — she competed at the Olympic level under conditions that would have broken many people, and she made a deliberate choice about how to live afterward. Both women were actors, not simply subjects acted upon.

Points of Contrast

Where They Diverge

The differences between Epstein and Gyarmati are as analytically productive as their similarities. The most significant difference is directional: Epstein fought to expand women's access to competitive swimming; Gyarmati exercised the right to leave it. This is not simply a biographical difference; it reflects genuinely different historical moments and different available forms of agency.

In Epstein's era, the fight was for inclusion. The question was whether women could compete at all, on what terms, in what clothing, for what recognition. Gyarmati's era had settled some of those questions — women's swimming was established at the Olympic level, women's records were recognized, women could wear functional racing suits. But new pressures had emerged: the professionalization and intensification of elite sport, the specific burdens of being the child of champions, and the context of Cold War international competition in which athletes represented national ideologies as much as individual excellence.

The two women also differ in the explicitness of their Jewish identity as a factor in their athletic careers. For Epstein, Jewish identity was interwoven with her advocacy in ways that Borish documents carefully — the dismantling of stereotypes about Jewish women's bodies was part of what her work accomplished, whether or not she always framed it that way explicitly. For Gyarmati, Jewish identity was a biographical fact with historical weight — she was a Jewish person at the Munich Olympics in 1972 — but its relationship to her athletic career was more indirect, filtered through family history, through the Holocaust's shadow over her parents' generation, and through the complex position of Jews in communist-era Hungary.

The Larger Argument

What This Reveals About Gender and Jewish Identity

Taken together, the stories of Epstein and Gyarmati, placed in the context Borish and Idels provide, illuminate a set of arguments about the relationship between gender, Jewish identity, and sport that extend well beyond swimming.

The first argument is about the politics of the body. Jewish women's access to sport has never been determined by individual talent alone. It has always been shaped by who controls athletic institutions, what kinds of bodies those institutions are designed to accommodate, and what assumptions about femininity and Jewish identity those institutions carry. Epstein's fight was not with bad individual actors; it was with structural arrangements that treated female athletic achievement as an anomaly to be managed rather than an excellence to be celebrated. Borish's scholarship shows that this was not Epstein's problem alone but a systemic feature of the athletic landscape that Jewish women navigated for decades.

"In the eyes of the Zionist revolution, the regeneration of the Jewish body was something that occurred in the tilling field and on the battlefield, but not on the sports field. Compared to the purposeful productivity of the halutz, the actions of the athlete appeared frivolous and empty. As a result, sporting heroes (whether male or female) and athletic achievements are absent from the Zionist collective memory of this time."
— Ofer Idels, "How to Lose Gracefully" (2022)

The second argument, supplied by Idels, is about the limits of the ideological frameworks that claimed to celebrate Jewish athletic achievement. The "New Jew" ideal — physically vigorous, nationally oriented, proudly strong — was built on an image of the Jewish male body and was systematically unable to accommodate Jewish women athletes on their own terms. The paradox Idels identifies — promoting strength while failing to celebrate the specific women who embodied it — is not simply a historical curiosity. It reflects a deeper pattern in which the achievements of Jewish women are absorbed into narratives about Jewish collective identity in ways that erase the individual women themselves.

The third argument concerns agency. Both Epstein and Gyarmati exercised agency within constraints they did not choose. Epstein worked within and against the AAU and the male-dominated athletic establishment. Gyarmati worked within and ultimately against the expectations created by her family legacy and national athletic culture. In both cases, the agency they exercised was meaningful precisely because it was constrained — because claiming space in the water required something more than simply being good enough to swim.

Synthesis

A Digital Academic Argument

The central argument of this project is that swimming, for Jewish women in the twentieth century, was never merely athletic. It was a site where gender and Jewish identity intersected with institutional power, cultural expectation, and historical trauma in ways that made every race, every record, and every decision to continue or to stop inherently political. Charlotte Epstein understood this when she fought the AAU over swimsuits in the 1910s and 1920s; Andrea Gyarmati lived it when she competed as a Jewish athlete at Munich in 1972. Together, their stories — read alongside the scholarship of Borish and Idels — make visible something that the history of sport has too often obscured: that the bodies of Jewish women in athletic spaces have always been contested terrain, and that the women who claimed that terrain anyway were doing something that deserves to be recognized not just as athletic achievement but as a form of resistance.
