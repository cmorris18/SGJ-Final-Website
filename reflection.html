Personal Reflection

Reflection

What the Water Taught Me

I have been swimming for most of my life. Since I was five years old, the pool has been a constant — the smell of chlorine, the cold shock of the first lap, the particular quiet that comes when your head goes under and the world outside disappears. I have had practices that broke me and practices that built me back up. I have almost quit. I have found my way back. I have competed at the college level at Lehigh, which was something I wanted badly enough to work for with everything I had. And for a few years after that, I stood on the other side of the lane rope and coached — watching younger swimmers work through the same cycles of struggle and discovery that I had worked through myself.

I say all of this not to make this project about me, but because honesty requires me to admit that when I chose to research Jewish swimmers for this final project, I thought I understood something about what I was getting into. Swimming was my sport. I knew what it felt like. That felt like a head start.

What I discovered is that I had understood almost nothing.

What Surprised Me

The Pool Is Not Neutral

The thing that surprised me most — the thing I keep coming back to — is how thoroughly I had naturalized my own experience in the sport. I had treated access to competitive swimming as a given. There was a team. There was a pool. I joined one and used the other. I struggled, but I struggled with the work of becoming a better athlete, not with whether I was permitted to be in the pool at all.

Reading about Charlotte Epstein's battles with the AAU over swimsuit regulations required me to sit with something uncomfortable: women had to fight, literally, for the right to wear clothing that would allow them to actually swim competitively. The AAU's rules were not about athletic performance. They were about controlling women's bodies, about deciding what female athletes were allowed to look like and how much of themselves they were allowed to expose in public. Those fights happened in the same sport I have practiced my entire life. They happened in pools that look, in some cases, exactly like the ones I've trained in.

"The physical and political emancipation went hand in hand for Epstein and her like-minded team-mates."
— Linda J. Borish, "The Cradle of American Champions" (2004)

When I was coaching, I thought of what I was passing on as technical knowledge: stroke mechanics, race strategy, how to read a split time. What this project has made me realize is that there is another tradition being transmitted whenever a swimmer stands in a starting block — a tradition of women having claimed that space, having refused to be excluded from it, having competed their way past every argument for why they didn't belong there. I was part of that transmission without knowing it. I didn't know yet what I was part of.

What I Learned

Privilege, History, and Honest Reckoning

One of the things I had to reckon with in this project is privilege. I entered competitive swimming without any of the barriers that Epstein's athletes faced. My gender was not a disqualifier. My religious or ethnic background did not make me a target for exclusion. The institutions I swam through — my club team, Lehigh's program — were designed to accommodate me without any particular effort on my part. I showed up, I worked hard, and the system worked for me.

This is not something to be ashamed of, but it is something to be clear-eyed about. The ease with which I accessed competitive swimming was not simply a reflection of my talent or my effort. It was also a reflection of the fact that people like me — people who weren't fighting institutional exclusion on the grounds of gender, religion, or ethnicity — have historically been the default beneficiary of athletic institutions. The fights that Epstein waged, the pressures that Gyarmati navigated, the institutional barriers that Borish documents — these were the conditions that made the pool I swam in possible. I should know that. I am glad, now, that I do.

I also learned something about the complexity of Jewish identity in athletic contexts that I had not considered before. I came into this project thinking of Jewish athletes primarily as people who had overcome antisemitism to achieve in sport. That is true, but it is incomplete. What Idels's scholarship on the "New Jew" paradox reveals is that Jewish athletic achievement has been not just a target of antisemitic dismissal but also an object of ideological appropriation within Jewish cultural politics — celebrated in the abstract, erased in the particular, and systematically stripped of its gendered complexity. Jewish women athletes were not simply fighting antisemitism. They were also navigating the expectations of their own communities, the frameworks of their own ideological traditions, the legacy of their own families.

Perspective Shift

How My Understanding Has Changed

I will be honest: before this project, the pool was where I went to compete, to train, to coach, to clear my head. It was personal and physical and not, in my mind, political. I understood in the abstract that sports and politics were connected — you learn that in history class, you see it on the news. But the pool, my pool, my sport, felt separate from all of that.

It is not separate. It has never been separate. The pool is a space where decisions about who belongs and who doesn't, whose body is acceptable and whose isn't, whose achievements count and whose are minimized — those decisions have always been made, enforced, and contested. Charlotte Epstein spent her life contesting them. Andrea Gyarmati carried them in her body every time she swam in international competition, and ultimately made a decision about how much of herself she was willing to give to a sport structured by demands she hadn't chosen.

What has changed for me is not my love of swimming — that remains, and if anything this project has deepened it, by connecting it to something larger than my own experience. What has changed is my ability to see the pool clearly, with its history included. When I think about my years coaching, I now think of them differently: I was part of a practice of transmission that stretches back a long time, to women who fought harder than I ever had to just to be in the water at all. I hope I passed something good on. I am more aware now of what I was part of.

Closing

Why This Project Matters, To Me

This project matters to me for reasons that are both academic and personal. Academically, it has pushed me to engage with the kind of intersectional analysis — where gender, religious/ethnic identity, and institutional power all operate together — that is central to what this course is about. I came in thinking I could separate those threads. The research made clear that they cannot be separated. Gender without Jewish identity, or Jewish identity without gender, or either without the institutional context Borish documents or the ideological framework Idels analyzes — none of those analyses would be sufficient. The whole picture requires all the threads held together.

Personally, it matters because it has given me a history for something I love. Swimming was, for a long time, just mine — a personal story, a private discipline, a thing between me and the water and the clock. Now I understand that it is also part of a much longer story, in which women have been claiming space in pools since Charlotte Epstein founded the WSA in 1917, and probably before. I am a very small part of a very long tradition.

That is both humbling and, honestly, something to be grateful for.
