Jewish Swimmers

Gender, Identity, and Resistance in the Water

Swimming has always felt like freedom to me. What I never considered was that for Jewish women in the twentieth century, the right to enter the water at all was something they had to fight for — politically, publicly, and at personal cost.

Personal Introduction

Why Swimming?

I started swimming when I was five years old. My parents signed me up for the neighborhood swim team, not because I begged them to, but because they decided it would be good for me. I didn't have much say in the matter. But somewhere between my first laps and the years that followed, something shifted. I fell in love with the sport in a way I hadn't anticipated.

It wasn't always smooth. On my first club team, the yardage was relentless, endless practices that seemed to be designed to wear you down before they built you up. There were stretches where I seriously considered walking away, and at one point, I almost quit. I ended up finding a new team that matched how I wanted to train and compete, which changed everything. I found my love of swimming again, and this time I made sure to give it my all.

That love carried me all the way to Lehigh University, where I swam competitively at the college level. The determination it took to get there, the focus, the sacrifice, the years of work, is something I'm still proud of. Beyond my effort to the sport itself, I was connected to the community, spending about three years coaching younger swimmers. In those years on the deck, I was passing something on, though I didn't fully understand yet what that something was.

I tell you all of this because when I began researching this project, I realized I had always treated the pool as neutral territory. A lane, a clock, a wall to push off from. What this research forced me to reckon with is that the pool is anything but neutral, and for Jewish women in the twentieth century, it never was.

"Charlotte Epstein serves as a symbol of the critical efforts of a Jewish sportswoman to improve the competitive opportunities and quest for physical emancipation of American women using their bodies in aquatic sports."
— Linda J. Borish, "The Cradle of American Champions" (2004)

Charlotte Epstein had to battle male-dominated institutions just for women to be allowed to compete at all. Andrea Gyarmati arrived at the 1972 Munich Olympics as a Jewish athlete, in the very city, in the very games, where eleven Israeli athletes were murdered by Palestinian terrorists. These women were not just athletes. They were navigating the intersection of gender, Jewish identity, and political history in one of the most contested spaces imaginable: the pool.

This project is my attempt to understand and express what the water really means, not just for me, but for the Jewish women who claimed it as a site of identity, resistance, and survival long before I ever touched a pool.
